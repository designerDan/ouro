Insights

There's an opportunity to focus less on image generation and more on image interpretation. My video feed of a pen dancing becomes an epic struggle between up and down to win the direction of hope.

There might be a market for chatGPT to webXR for disaster preparedness training, corporations have risk mitigation departments, or for ER training. Like, 'I'm looking to create a training for earthquake preparedness.' Eleven labs would feed the speech.

'Create stunning visual content with minimal effort' sums up the affordances for these softwares.

The interfaces that use code are for developers who want a one and done, not visual people who want to mess with the image and create a meaningful specific.

Image generation and image editing are two separate categories. Image editing involves image generation but image generation does not involve image editing. Improving image generation is improving the model and the generative inputs. Editing is something different entirely. Most softwares incorporate the two but they are not exclusive to each other.

Few softwares have managed to make an interface that doesn't just explain the software or throw a bunch of sliders and knobs at you but instead condense and exposes that information to an audience in a relatable and contextually relevant manner.

Sketch to AI interface is the current gap.

Interface

The most common interface is a canvas with left and right flyouts. A user input x AI output side by side display is a common canvas pattern. It is not uncommon for image generation software to have a Discord like interface.

Thinking mathematically and linearly we often get inputs on the left, results in the middle, and deviations/variations on the right. Though this is not exhaustive. The most common derivation is tools in the left flyout.

The interfaces range from technical to casual in how much information the user is presented. It's like some interfaces are trying to be informative, datic, and educational over intuitive.
